Protecting your personal privacy and reputation with social media

Thu, 08 Dec 2011

Posted In: web

Tagged: strategyprivacy

Personal privacy online is a hot topic in today's social media world. Facebook's privacy settings are notoriously complicated and constantly changing. Google Plus and Twitter use simpler - but radically different - paradigms for user privacy, and location-based networks such as Foursquare introduce other privacy concerns. Reputation management is closely tied in with privacy as well; suppose that a Facebook friend tags you in a potentially incriminating photo.

Because of the complexity of trying to manage "who can see what", especially on multiple social networks, I personally favor a "keep it simple" approach:

Post everything publicly; and assume everything you post is public.

So what does this mean?

Choose carefully what you post online, and post it publicly

Only post a status, photo, or video if you're OK with the whole world seeing it. If you're not OK with that, don't post it. Remember that anything you post CAN BE directly or indirectly shared with the whole world. Might as well put it out there to start with, right?

Whatever you choose to post can (and likely will) be viewed in a job search, by a co-worker or boss, pastor, potential date, parents, family, and more. It's a no-brainer that you need to think about what you post instead of relying on Facebook to protect it.

Additionally, when I post publicly, it removes the temptation to post something that could be harmful or hurtful if it gets out. For example, I'd love to post horror stories about some of my web design clients. But that's professionally a bad idea and the story could make its way to those people.

If you have something private to discuss with another person, Twitter & Facebook both have good private-messaging systems. Email and the phone work too.

Also: Check over your Facebook profile. Is your phone number shared publicly, or just with friends? What's your current job, and who can see that? Most of the Facebook setttings are "Friends", "Friends of Friends", or "Everyone". Choose carefully - but remember that even if you put restricted settings on some parts of your profile, they can still get out there if someone shares them. Are you OK with that? If not, take it down and hope nobody has already shared it.

Try to control what other people can say about you

Now what you're all waiting for - actually digging into Facebook's privacy settings. Click the little triangle at the top right of your Facebook page and choose "Privacy Settings". Make sure - like above - your default status is "public".

As you can see, the idea here is that anything I post is public (that is, filtered before I post it). Then, I restrict others from tagging me in photos, checking me in to places (even if I'm not there), etc. - so it reduces the risk that someone can negatively impact my reputation in this way.

Kudos to Facebook for seriously revamping this interface - but it's still needlessly complicated.

Also: Be careful of approving Facebook apps since they can access and share your information. I got an app request the other day for some birthday calendar thing. Uh, no thanks.

Privacy is your responsibility

Don't rely on Facebook, Twitter, or Google+ to protect your privacy. Anyone can share your information from those sites with the whole world - and we haven't even mentioned what these large companies may do with your data. Did you know that all public tweets are archived in the Library of Congress?

I always assume anything that goes online is out of my control - therefore, I need to control it before it gets there. Posting my status publicly helps me to do that and it puts me in the right mindset when I hit that "Post" button.

Do you have anything to add? Write a comment! Of course, remember, everyone can see it!
